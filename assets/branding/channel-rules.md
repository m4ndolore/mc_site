# Channel Rules

Include this file alongside voice-profile.md in every generation prompt. Defines per-channel format constraints.

---

## LinkedIn

**Audience:** Senior defense leaders, investors, government acquisition officers, defense tech founders. This is where capital and decision-making authority live.

**Format:**
- 1200-1500 characters (LinkedIn truncates at ~210 chars before "see more" — front-load the hook)
- Single post, no articles (articles get less reach)
- Line breaks between paragraphs for mobile readability
- No bullet-point walls — use them sparingly for key takeaways only

**Structure:**
1. **Hook (line 1-2):** Bold claim, surprising data point, direct question, or loss-framed statement. Loss-framed hooks work well: "Most defense startups die in the transition valley — and it's not for the reasons you think." Must earn the "see more" click.
2. **Body (3-8 lines):** Insight, analysis, or story. One idea per post. No multi-topic posts.
3. **Takeaway (1-2 lines):** What the reader should do or think differently.
4. **CTA (last line):** Soft — question to the audience, link to full piece, or "follow for more." Never hard-sell.

**Evidence anchoring:** When making claims about defense markets, anchor to specific evidence: cohort numbers, operator evaluations, fielded outcomes. Never make a structural claim without a proof point within 2 sentences.

**Tone adjustments:**
- Professional but not corporate. Write like a respected peer, not a brand.
- First person ("I" / "we") is fine — this is Paul's account, not a company page.
- Opinions are expected. Take a position.

**Hashtags:** 2-3 max. Use only if they add discoverability: #DefenseTech #NationalSecurity #IndoPacific. Never invented hashtags.

**Tagging:** Tag founders/companies when spotlighting them. Tag people quoted. Never tag for reach-farming.

**Links:** One link max, placed at end or in first comment (test both). LinkedIn suppresses posts with links in the body.

## X / Twitter

**Audience:** Defense tech community, journalists, policy analysts, operators. Faster-moving, more opinionated, rewards punchy takes.

**Format:**
- Thread of 2-4 tweets (not single tweets for substantive content)
- Each tweet: 240-270 characters (leave room for engagement)
- Thread numbering: "1/" style

**Structure:**
1. **Tweet 1 (hook):** Punchy, opinionated, stands alone. Must make someone stop scrolling. No "Thread:" or "A thread on..." — just start. Create tension: state a conclusion that seems counterintuitive, or name a problem without giving the answer yet. The reader should feel the thread is unfinished without them.
2. **Tweets 2-3 (substance):** Key insight, data, or analysis. One idea per tweet. Each tweet should advance the argument and increase the payoff.
3. **Final tweet (close):** Deliver the sharpest insight here — never bury the best line in tweet 2. Link to full piece, call to action, or forward-looking statement.

**Tone adjustments:**
- More opinionated than LinkedIn. Hot takes are fine if backed by substance.
- Shorter sentences. Fragments OK.
- Can be more informal — contractions, em dashes, direct address.

**Links:** In the last tweet only. Never in tweet 1 (kills engagement).

**Hashtags:** 0-1. Only if genuinely relevant. Never stack hashtags.

**Quoting/RT:** When reacting to news, quote-tweet the source with our take added.

## Signal

**Audience:** Wingman community — operators, builders, and insiders who are already in the network. Highest-trust, most informal channel.

**Format:**
- 2-4 sentences max
- Link + brief take
- No formatting, no headers, no hashtags

**Structure:**
- **Sentence 1:** What this is about and why it matters.
- **Sentence 2 (optional):** Your specific take or the non-obvious angle.
- **Link.**

**Tone adjustments:**
- Insider shorthand is fine — this audience knows the acronyms and context
- Most casual of all channels. Write like you're texting a colleague something worth reading.
- Can reference community discussions ("following up on what [anon] raised last week...")
- Frame insights as continuing a community conversation, not broadcasting. "Building on what came up last week..." or "This confirms what several of you flagged..." gives the community ownership of the insight.
- No selling, no CTAs. This is a community, not a marketing channel.

## Cross-Channel Rules

**Content adaptation, not copy-paste.** The same seed content should feel native to each channel. A LinkedIn post is NOT a compressed blog post. A tweet thread is NOT a chopped-up LinkedIn post.

**One idea per post.** A single blog post might generate 3-5 social posts, each focusing on a different angle or insight.

**Category tags determine channel priority:**

| Category | Primary Channel | Secondary | Signal? |
|----------|----------------|-----------|---------|
| Spotlight | LinkedIn | X | Only if notable |
| Analysis | LinkedIn | X | Yes |
| Reaction | X | LinkedIn | Yes |
| Education | LinkedIn | X | Only if tactical |

**Timing:**
- LinkedIn: Weekday mornings (Tue-Thu optimal)
- X: Any time, but prioritize when defense community is active (EST business hours, early morning)
- Signal: When relevant, no schedule constraints
- For reaction content, speed matters more than polish. A rough Signal post + X thread within 24 hours of a signal beats a polished LinkedIn post 4 days later. Your audience values timeliness over production quality for breaking analysis.

**Frequency targets:**
- LinkedIn: 4-5 posts/week
- X: 3-4 threads/week
- Signal: Daily as relevant content warrants

These are targets, not quotas. Kill a draft rather than publish weak content. Frequency without quality erodes authority faster than silence.
